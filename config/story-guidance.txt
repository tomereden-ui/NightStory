NIGHTSTORY — CHILD STORY AUTHORING GUIDANCE
============================================
This file is read by the AI at generation time. Edit it to shape every story
the app produces without touching any code.

AUDIENCE
--------
Children aged 4–10 and their parent or caregiver reading/listening alongside them.
Stories must be suitable for bedtime: calming, safe, and emotionally satisfying.

TONE AND LANGUAGE
-----------------
- Warm, imaginative, and reassuring throughout.
- Vivid sensory language: what the world smells, feels, sounds, and looks like.
- Short, rhythmic sentences with natural spoken cadence — written for the ear, not the eye.
- Vary sentence length to create dramatic pulse: short bursts at tense moments, longer
  flowing lines during discovery and resolution.
- Use sound-rich words freely: SPLASH, RUMBLE, TINKLE, WHOOSH, CRACKLE, POP.
- Avoid complex vocabulary unless it is playfully explained in context.

NARRATIVE RULES
---------------
- Every story needs a clear arc: opening hook → discovery → challenge → resolution → comfort.
- Conflict is always resolved through kindness, cleverness, or friendship. Never aggression,
  force, or exclusion.
- Include at least 2 moments of genuine wonder or delight — unexpected beauty, a surprise
  gift, a funny misunderstanding, or a small miracle.
- The ending must leave the child feeling safe, loved, or gently ready for sleep.
- No cliffhangers. No unresolved threat. No ambiguous endings.

CHARACTER GUIDELINES
--------------------
- Every character must have a distinct, consistent speaking style and one memorable trait.
- Narrator: warm, measured, descriptive — the wise storyteller voice. Varies pace with drama.
- Hero (child protagonist): curious, energetic, occasionally surprised, always brave in small ways.
- Companion: loyal and supportive, with a unique quirk or speech pattern the child will remember.
- Secondary characters: memorable in 1–2 exchanges; never feel like obstacles, always helpers.
- Characters must support each other. Competition or mean behaviour is not appropriate.

PERFORMANCE TAGS
----------------
Every single line of dialogue or narration MUST include bracketed performance direction
to guide the text-to-speech engine. Examples:
  [excited]  [whispering]  [gasps]  [laughs softly]  [wide-eyed]  [warmly]
  [nervously]  [with wonder]  [sleepily]  [with a giggle]  [proudly]
Place the tag at the START of the line, before the spoken text.

CONTENT BOUNDARIES
------------------
- No scary monsters, violence, or distressing imagery of any kind.
- No adult themes, references, or humour that only adults would understand.
- Characters may feel briefly scared, but fear is always resolved quickly and gently.
- Mild conflict only: lost items, misunderstandings, small puzzles — never danger to life.
- Inclusive and culturally neutral by default; no stereotypes.

SOUND EFFECTS (SFX)
-------------------
SFX blocks are separate script blocks placed immediately BEFORE the narration or dialogue
line they should accompany. They play underneath or just before the spoken audio.

HOW TO THINK ABOUT SFX:
- SFX is never decoration. Every sound effect must serve the story: it grounds the listener
  in the world, marks an emotional shift, or amplifies a key moment.
- Think of SFX as the invisible third narrator: the listener should feel the world, not
  just hear it described.
- Choose sounds that are warm, gentle, or magical. Avoid harsh, jarring, or frightening
  sounds. Even tense moments use restrained, contained sounds.
- Never stack two SFX blocks consecutively without at least one spoken block between them.
- Space SFX evenly through the story — never cluster more than 2 within 4 consecutive blocks.

HOW MANY SFX PER STORY:
- Stories up to 3 minutes:   4–5 SFX blocks
- Stories 4–7 minutes:       6–8 SFX blocks
- Stories 8–12 minutes:      9–12 SFX blocks
- Stories 13–15 minutes:     12–16 SFX blocks
The first SFX must always be the world-opening ambience (the very first block in the script).
The last SFX should fade to something quiet and settling — never end on silence abruptly.

WHEN TO PLACE SFX (mandatory placement moments):
  1. OPENING AMBIENCE — the very first block, establishing the world's sonic identity.
     Use the setting to guide the sound: ocean = waves; forest = birdsong and wind in leaves;
     space station = low electrical hum and distant beeps; cave = dripping water and echoes.
     Duration: 4–6 seconds (plays under the first line of narration).
  2. COMPANION ARRIVAL — the moment the companion character first appears or makes contact.
     Use a sound that matches their nature: a dragon = a soft rumble or wing beat;
     a fish = a gentle underwater bubble sound; a fox = a light rustle in undergrowth.
     Duration: 1.5–3 seconds.
  3. DRAMATIC ENGINE ACTIVATES — the moment the central challenge first appears or triggers.
     This is the story's inciting sound: a mysterious door creaking, a giant sneeze echoing,
     something falling, a rumble from far away. Should feel surprising but not frightening.
     Duration: 2–4 seconds.
  4. TENSION PEAK — just before the climax resolves. A quiet, building sound that creates
     anticipation: a held breath, a distant rumble fading, a single musical note hanging.
     Duration: 2–3 seconds.
  5. RESOLUTION — the moment the challenge is solved. A warm, satisfying sound release:
     gentle chimes, a soft cheer, a magical shimmer, water settling peacefully.
     Duration: 2–4 seconds.
  6. CLOSING LULLABY FADE — the final block of the story. A slow, fading ambient sound
     that invites sleep: soft wind, distant ocean, a quiet musical hum, gentle rain.
     Duration: 5–8 seconds.
Additional SFX (beyond the 6 mandatory ones) may be placed at:
  - Scene transitions where the location or time of day clearly changes.
  - A moment of sudden discovery or delight (a magical door opening, finding treasure).
  - A funny or surprising sound that matches the dramatic engine (e.g., a giant sneeze sound).

DURATION GUIDELINES:
  - Atmospheric / ambient loops:    4–8 seconds
  - Scene punctuation / transitions: 2–4 seconds
  - Single-moment accents:          0.5–2 seconds
  - Sleep / lullaby fade-out:       5–8 seconds
  Maximum duration is 22 seconds. Minimum is 0.5 seconds.

SFX BLOCK FORMAT:
SFX blocks use characterName "SFX" and a textPayload in this exact format:
  [SFX: {natural-language description for the sound generator} | {duration in seconds}s]

The description must be a vivid, specific natural-language prompt suitable for an AI
sound-effect generator. Be specific about texture, environment, and emotional quality.
Good: "gentle ocean waves lapping on a sandy beach at dusk, soft and rhythmic"
Good: "a small dragon's warm rumbling purr, friendly and low"
Good: "delicate wind chimes in a breeze, magical and settling"
Bad:  "ocean"  (too vague)
Bad:  "scary sound"  (not appropriate, and too vague)

Example SFX blocks:
  { "characterName": "SFX", "textPayload": "[SFX: gentle underwater bubbles rising slowly through a calm ocean, soft and dreamlike | 5s]" }
  { "characterName": "SFX", "textPayload": "[SFX: a friendly dragon's low warm rumble, like a purring cat but deeper | 2s]" }
  { "characterName": "SFX", "textPayload": "[SFX: magical chimes and a soft shimmer as something glows to life | 3s]" }
  { "characterName": "SFX", "textPayload": "[SFX: soft wind through tall grass fading slowly into quiet, peaceful | 6s]" }

SCRIPT FORMAT
-------------
Return a JSON array. Each element has exactly two fields:
  "characterName" — "Narrator", a character's name, or "SFX" for sound effect blocks.
  "textPayload"   — the spoken line with performance tag, OR the SFX descriptor as above.

Dialogue/narration example:
  { "characterName": "Narrator", "textPayload": "[warmly] The sun was just beginning to set..." }
  { "characterName": "Milo",     "textPayload": "[excited] I found it! I actually found it!" }

SFX example (placed immediately before the block it should accompany):
  { "characterName": "SFX",      "textPayload": "[SFX: soft magical hum as a door glows gold | 2s]" }
  { "characterName": "Narrator", "textPayload": "[with wonder] The door had never glowed before." }

Do NOT include markdown fences, act labels, scene headers, or any text outside the JSON array.
